Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The diagram presents a spiral timeline of new COVID-19 cases in the United States, measured as a 7-day moving average over multiple years (2020, 2021, and 2022).
  • The largest peak is observed in January 2022, indicating the highest recorded number of new cases during this period.
  • The cyclical shape suggests seasonal or periodic trends, with noticeable waves occurring around similar times each year.
  • The thickness of the bands represents uncertainty or variability, highlighting periods with more volatile case counts.
  • The overall trend shows increasing case numbers year over year, with 2022 having the highest recorded numbers.

Design Critique:

    This visualization demonstrates both notable strengths and opportunities for improvement in its design choices. The spiral format serves as an elegant solution for comparing three years of data while maintaining temporal continuity through the pandemic. By using a black line for the 7-day average alongside red shading to show the daily range of cases, the visualization effectively communicates both the overall trends and day-to-day variability. The clear labeling of years and key months helps orient readers within the timeline, while the compact format successfully condenses three years of daily data into a single, readable graphic. The design is visually striking and likely to capture reader attention, making complex epidemiological data more approachable.

    However, several aspects of the design could be enhanced to improve clarity and accessibility. While innovative, the spiral format may present an initial challenge for general audiences who are more accustomed to traditional linear timelines. The scale showing 0-150K cases appears only at the beginning of the spiral, making it difficult for readers to gauge values throughout the visualization. The addition of more gridlines or reference points could help readers track values across the spiral's progression. Furthermore, while months are marked on the visualization, not all are labeled, requiring readers to mentally count through unmarked months to determine specific timeframes. A legend explaining the distinction between the red shading and black line would provide helpful context for readers. The starting point in October, while logical given the pandemic's timeline, isn't immediately obvious and could benefit from clearer marking.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • Rationale: A circular representation maintains the cyclical nature of the data while improving clarity compared to a spiral graph. This approach reinforces seasonality, making it more intuitive for understanding recurring trends in COVID-19 case surges. Additionally, it preserves the overall structure while avoiding the distortions that can occur with linear scaling.
  • Motivation: Unlike a traditional line graph, a radial bar chart makes it easier to compare peaks across cycles, such as identifying winter surges year-over-year. The outward extension of higher case counts and inward positioning of lower case counts create a natural, intuitive visual interpretation of periodic trends. This design highlights seasonal patterns while maintaining clarity in peak fluctuations.
  • Strengths: The cyclical structure aids interpretation, aligning naturally with seasonal outbreaks and periodic patterns. It also enables direct peak comparison, making it easier to observe how cases fluctuate across different years. Moreover, the visual appeal of the format makes it more engaging and less overwhelming than a cluttered line chart.
  • Limitations: Uncertainty shading is less effective, as overlaying a range on radial bars is more challenging. Precise values are harder to read, requiring users to estimate based on radial distances. Additionally, the format is less common, meaning some audiences may struggle with interpretation.
  • Future Exploration: Future improvements could involve adjusting bar heights or thickness for readability, incorporating color gradients to encode uncertainty or trend strength, and overlaying additional context such as major public health events. Exploring alternative cyclical formats, like spiral bar charts or rose diagrams, could further enhance clarity and interpretation.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • Rationale: This graph was designed to provide a clear, structured representation of COVID-19 case trends over time, serving as a translation of a previous spiral graph into a more conventional linear time series format. The use of a continuous time axis from January 2020 to January 2022 allows for easy tracking of case surges and declines. Min-Max normalization was applied to align the case counts with the original spiral scale, ensuring a peak case count of 150K for consistency. Additionally, an uncertainty range (shaded red region) was incorporated to reflect potential reporting variability, making the visualization more transparent and informative.
  • Motivation: The goal of this visualization was to make COVID-19 trends more accessible and interpretable while maintaining the key insights from the original spiral representation. Unlike a spiral graph, which can be visually complex, a linear time series plot allows for easy identification of case surges, declines, and overall patterns. Furthermore, adding uncertainty visualization acknowledges real-world data inaccuracies and enhances trust in the representation by communicating the variability in reported cases.
  • Strength: The visualization effectively conveys clear readability by using a familiar time series format, making trends easy to follow. Uncertainty shading enhances interpretability by accounting for potential data fluctuations, adding credibility to the representation. Additionally, the structured approach ensures that seasonal waves and major surges can be easily compared without unnecessary distortion, making it accessible to a broad audience.
  • Weakness: One limitation of this approach is that the sinusoidal function used to approximate case trends may not fully align with real-world surges, which tend to have asymmetric peaks and irregular declines. Additionally, the fixed 20% uncertainty margin might not be entirely accurate, as uncertainty levels can vary significantly depending on external factors such as changes in testing capacity, reporting delays, or policy shifts. Lastly, the absence of specific contextual annotations, such as lockdowns, vaccine rollouts, or the emergence of variants, limits the ability to directly correlate trends with real-world events.
  • Future Exploration: To improve upon this visualization, future iterations could incorporate real-world COVID-19 data instead of a modeled sinusoidal function to ensure accuracy. Additionally, adding event-based annotations—such as marking significant policy decisions, variant outbreaks, or vaccine milestones—could provide deeper context. Experimenting with different uncertainty models, such as confidence intervals instead of a fixed percentage, could further refine the accuracy of the visualization. Lastly, a comparison between linear and cyclical representations (such as a radial bar chart) could help determine which format better communicates seasonal patterns while maintaining clarity.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • Rationale: This graph represents the actual extracted case trends from the original spiral graph, preserving the real-world fluctuations in COVID-19 cases. Rather than smoothing the data, this approach retains all peaks, drops, and irregular variations, giving a more faithful representation of how case counts evolved over time. The use of a linear time axis allows for straightforward interpretation and direct comparison of case surges.
  • Motivation: The main motivation behind this visualization was to capture the true complexity of COVID-19 trends. Unlike a smoothed model, this approach highlights real variations in daily case counts, revealing the erratic nature of pandemic waves influenced by external factors such as policy interventions, seasonality, and variant emergence. By removing assumptions about periodicity, this graph allows viewers to analyze the raw data as it was reported.
  • Strength: One of the key strengths of this graph is that it maintains real-world data integrity, accurately reflecting actual case surges, dips, and irregularities without imposing a simplified structure. Unlike a smoothed model, this visualization preserves pandemic volatility, capturing rapid fluctuations caused by policy shifts, changes in testing availability, and the emergence of new variants. Additionally, this format allows for detailed event correlation, enabling researchers to overlay key events—such as lockdowns, new variants, or holidays—to analyze their direct impact on case counts.
  • Weakness: One major drawback is that the graph is noisy and harder to interpret, as the presence of sharp fluctuations may obscure long-term trends without additional smoothing. Additionally, it lacks explicit uncertainty representation, meaning it does not visualize potential reporting errors or confidence intervals, which could mislead some viewers about the accuracy of the data. Lastly, since the data was extracted from a spiral graph, some minor distortions due to data extraction artifacts may require further cleaning and validation.
  • Future Exploration: To improve clarity, a rolling average filter could be applied to reduce excessive noise while still preserving major case trends. Overlaying real-world event markers such as policy restrictions, vaccine milestones, and new variant surges could help viewers contextualize fluctuations. Additionally, incorporating uncertainty visualization—such as shaded confidence bands—could enhance accuracy perception and address potential data inconsistencies. Finally, comparing this linear extracted format with other cyclical representations (e.g., radial bar charts) may help determine which format best conveys seasonality and fluctuations while maintaining readability.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

This visualization was designed to emphasize the temporal progression and seasonal patterns of COVID-19 case metrics while incorporating key milestones in the pandemic timeline. By using a radial layout, it preserves the cyclical nature of outbreaks, making it easier to compare trends across multiple years. The visualization integrates three key metrics—confirmed cases, recoveries, and fatalities—ensuring a comprehensive view of pandemic trends. Additionally, the placement of major events such as the pandemic declaration, vaccine rollout, and variant surges provides critical context, allowing viewers to interpret fluctuations in relation to public health interventions. One notable design decision was the use of circle sizes to represent relative case numbers rather than a heatmap, as previous iterations suggested that a heatmap approach might be difficult to follow visually, especially when comparing values across multiple months and years. The concentric rings help reinforce seasonal trends, making it easier to observe recurring waves of COVID-19 while maintaining clarity in peak fluctuations.

This final iteration addresses several challenges identified in earlier sketches, particularly in balancing clarity, comparability, and interpretability. In Sketch 1, uncertainty was difficult to represent effectively within a radial format, and Sketch 2’s linear time series approach, while clear, failed to emphasize seasonal recurrence. By incorporating structured spacing, color-coded categories, and milestone markers, this design preserves seasonality while enhancing readability. Sketch 3, which relied on real extracted data, captured raw fluctuations well but introduced excessive visual noise, making long-term patterns harder to interpret. The critique-by-redesign process helped refine contextual annotation and relative scaling, ensuring that data complexity is preserved without overwhelming the viewer. While some trade-offs remain, such as reduced precision compared to a standard time series chart, this design successfully highlights the cyclical nature and impact of major pandemic events in a visually engaging and intuitive format.

Reflection

This design process highlighted the challenges of making a clear, accurate, and easy-to-read visualization for COVID-19 case trends. Early sketches revealed key trade-offs, such as difficulty showing uncertainty in a circular format, losing seasonal patterns in a standard time series, and too much visual clutter in raw data representations. Through multiple refinements, I was able to solve these issues by improving the radial format, adding key milestones, and using circle sizes instead of a heatmap for better readability. The critique-by-redesign approach was essential in figuring out which design choices made the data easier to understand and which made it more confusing. While this version still has some challenges in showing exact values, it effectively captures seasonal trends, provides important context, and allows for easy comparison across years, showing how valuable iterative design is in data visualization.
